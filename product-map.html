Product Vision

The problem, the solution, and why this spreads on its own

📸

The Problem

Cleaning crews photograph every job. Thousands of photos disappear into group chats, personal phones, and cloud folders no one organizes. Facility managers have no visibility. Companies have no liability protection. The proof of excellent work simply evaporates.

📋

The Proof Gap

When a client questions whether a job was done, a cleaning company has nothing. No structured record, no timestamped documentation, no compliance evidence. One complaint becomes a lost contract. One incident becomes a lawsuit. The gap between "we cleaned it" and "we can prove we cleaned it" is where revenue dies.

⚡

The Opportunity

Commercial cleaning companies with 1–10 crews ($500k–$5M revenue) are underserved by software. They can't afford enterprise BSM platforms. They need something that starts with their existing workflow — photos via phone — and builds from there.

🔄

Two Input Modes

MVP1: Ad-hoc photo flow — crew sends photos + captions, AI structures them into a report.

MVP2: Structured task lists — crew works area by area through a predefined checklist, attaches photos per area, report auto-assembles.

💡

The Solution (one sentence)

A Telegram-native platform that turns crew photos into branded post-cleaning reports, structured compliance documentation, and reusable marketing assets — delivered automatically to facility managers after every job.

🚀

The K-Factor Mechanic — Built-in Viral Distribution

Every report delivered to a facility manager carries a "Powered by [Product Name]" badge. The facility manager sees it. Shows it to their colleagues. Passes it to their network. Each report is a product demo delivered to a qualified prospect — at zero acquisition cost.

With 60+ recurring clients at pilot alone, the badge lands in 60+ inboxes every cleaning cycle. That's thousands of impressions per month from one customer.

🪨 Stone Floors ▼

Polished Marble (Carrara, Calacatta, Emperador)

▼
Surface Category

Natural Stone — Calcite-based

Carrara: White/grey, fine grain, relatively affordable. Calacatta: Bright white with dramatic gold/grey veining, premium pricing. Emperador: Dark brown marble, Spanish origin, highly sensitive to acid.

Approved Chemicals
  • Stone-specific neutral cleaner, pH 7.0–8.0, dilution 1:50, dwell time 2 min
  • Distilled or deionized water rinse
  • Marble-safe impregnating sealer (annual application)
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Any acid-based cleaner (pH <7) — vinegar, citrus, bathroom descalers
  • Bleach / sodium hypochlorite — discolors veining
  • Ammonia-based cleaners — dull polish
  • Multi-surface cleaners (usually pH 4–6)
  • Abrasive scrubbing compounds
Equipment
  • Microfiber flat mop (white or yellow pad only)
  • Two-bucket system: solution + rinse
  • Soft microfiber buffing cloth for drying
  • No rotary machines unless equipped with diamond pads for polish maintenance (specialist only)
Process
  1. Dry dust mop to remove grit (grit = scratch risk)
  2. Mix neutral stone cleaner at 1:50 in bucket
  3. Damp mop in overlapping passes — do not flood
  4. Rinse with clean water (second bucket)
  5. Dry buff immediately with microfiber — no standing water
  6. Inspect for etch marks or dull spots — report immediately
Timing

Dwell time: 2 minutes max for cleaner

Dry time: Buff dry immediately — do not allow air-dry (water spots etch over time)

Frequency: Daily damp mop; deep clean monthly; seal annually

Preventive Maintenance
  • Apply impregnating sealer annually (or bi-annually in high-traffic)
  • Place walk-off mats at all entrances to reduce grit ingress
  • Address spills immediately — liquid dwell = etch risk
  • Repolish with diamond pad system every 2–3 years (specialist service)
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Acidic cleaners (pH <7) on marble etch the surface within 30 seconds of contact, creating a permanent dull spot visible from 10 feet. Professional re-honing costs $8–$25/sq ft. A single misapplication on a $50,000 lobby is a liability event.
  • Calacatta is more porous than Carrara — etching risk is higher. Emperador is the most sensitive; even short acid exposure causes visible damage.
  • Ammonia-based glass cleaners used on marble surfaces (common error when cleaning adjacent glass) will gradually degrade the polish — damage is cumulative and irreversible without re-honing.
  • Wet mop left sitting on polished marble overnight will leave tide marks requiring professional buffing to remove.

Honed Marble

▼
Surface Category

Natural Stone — Calcite-based, matte finish

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral stone cleaner (7.0–8.0), 1:50, 2 min dwell
  • Distilled water rinse
Prohibited Chemicals
  • All acidic cleaners — same risk as polished but etching is LESS visible on honed initially, leading to progressive undetected damage
  • Oil-based soaps — honed marble absorbs oils, darkens permanently
  • Wax/polish products — not compatible with honed finish
Equipment & Process
  • Microfiber flat mop (same as polished)
  • Dust mop first to remove grit
  • Damp mop with neutral solution
  • Rinse and buff dry
  • Honed surfaces show fingerprints and water spots more on dark colorways — buff thoroughly
Timing & Frequency

Dwell: 2 min max

Frequency: Daily damp mop; seal more frequently than polished (honed is more porous)

Preventive Maintenance
  • Seal every 6 months (more frequently than polished)
  • Penetrating sealer only — no topical sealers
  • Address spills faster than polished marble
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Honed marble hides early acid etching — crews may not notice damage until it becomes severe. By then, re-honing the entire floor is required, not just spot treatment.
  • Oil-based soaps (common in hospitality settings) permanently darken honed marble. There is no chemical fix — the oil must be professionally drawn out with a poultice, and even then results are inconsistent.

Travertine (Filled vs Unfilled)

▼
Surface Category

Natural Stone — Limestone family, highly porous, often has surface voids (filled with grout or left open)

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral stone cleaner (7.0–8.0), 1:50
  • Travertine-specific rinse-free cleaner for daily maintenance
Prohibited Chemicals
  • All acids — travertine is calcite-based, highly reactive to acid
  • Alkaline cleaners above pH 10 — damages fillers in filled travertine
  • Steam cleaning on unfilled travertine — forces water into voids
Equipment & Process
  • Dust mop — critical as grit scratches soft surface
  • Damp microfiber mop with very light moisture
  • Unfilled: use minimal water, dry immediately
  • Filled: standard damp mop acceptable
  • Blot spills immediately
Frequency

Daily dust mop; damp mop 2–3x/week; seal every 6 months

Preventive Maintenance
  • Penetrating sealer every 6 months
  • Inspect and re-grout filled holes annually
  • Walk-off mats mandatory at all entries
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Unfilled travertine with standing water will absorb moisture into voids, causing subsurface damage and eventual cracking of the stone. Never wet mop and leave.
  • Travertine is softer than marble (Mohs 3–4). Grit from shoes that gets trapped under a mop pad acts like sandpaper — always dust mop first without exception.

Limestone

▼
Surface Category

Natural Stone — Sedimentary, calcite-based, very soft (Mohs 3). Most acid-sensitive of all commercial stone floors.

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral stone cleaner strictly 7.0–8.5
  • Rinse with distilled water only (tap water minerals leave deposits on limestone)
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Any acid — will etch faster than marble or travertine
  • Hard water (use distilled for rinsing)
  • Alkaline degreasers above pH 9
  • Wax or polish
Process
  1. Dust mop
  2. Damp mop with neutral solution (barely damp)
  3. Rinse with distilled water
  4. Dry immediately
Frequency

Daily dust mop; light damp mop weekly; seal every 6–12 months

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Limestone is the most vulnerable commercial stone — even brief contact with citrus-based hand soap tracked onto the floor will etch visible marks within minutes. All adjacent areas (reception, café, lobby) must use non-acid products without exception.
  • Tap water with high mineral content leaves calcium deposits on limestone that are extremely difficult to remove without acid (which damages the stone). Use distilled water for all rinse steps.

Granite (Polished vs Flamed)

▼
Surface Category

Natural Stone — Igneous, silica-based (Mohs 6–7). Much harder and more durable than calcite stones. More forgiving with cleaning chemicals but not immune.

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral to mildly alkaline (7.0–9.5), 1:50
  • Stone-safe degreaser at pH 9.0–9.5 for kitchen areas
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Bleach over 5% concentration — can affect sealant
  • Strong acids — technically tolerated short-term but damages sealant and over time pits surface
  • Oil-based cleaners on polished — residue attracts soil
Process — Polished
  1. Dust mop
  2. Damp mop with neutral/mild alkaline solution
  3. Rinse and dry
  4. Buff with dry microfiber for shine
Process — Flamed
  • Flamed finish is rougher, textured surface — use stiffer scrub brush for soiled areas
  • Allow longer dwell on heavy soiling (5 min)
  • Rinse thoroughly — rough texture traps cleaner residue
Frequency

Daily damp mop; seal annually (polished); flamed may not require sealing depending on finish

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Flamed granite's rough texture traps cleaning product residue — inadequate rinsing leaves a sticky film that attracts more soil and defeats the purpose of cleaning.
  • Granite often installed near marble (mixed stone lobbies) — ensure crew distinguishes surfaces and uses appropriate products for each. Using marble cleaner on granite is fine; using granite degreaser on marble is a liability event.

Slate

▼
Surface Category

Natural Stone — Metamorphic, silica/clay-based. Cleft (natural split) or honed surface. Naturally non-slip. Common in commercial entryways.

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral cleaner (6.5–9.0)
  • Mild alkaline for degreasing (pH 8–9.5)
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Wax or acrylic floor finish — seals into the cleft surface unevenly, peels
  • Strong oxidizing agents
Process
  1. Dust mop or vacuum (cleft surface traps debris)
  2. Scrub with neutral solution and stiff brush on heavily soiled areas
  3. Damp mop overall
  4. Rinse thoroughly
  5. Allow to air dry — slate is naturally non-slip even wet
Frequency

Daily sweep/vacuum; weekly damp mop; seal annually with penetrating sealer

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Applying wax or floor finish to slate produces an uneven, patchy appearance that is extremely difficult to strip. The cleft texture prevents even application and film-forming products peel within weeks.
  • Some slate (particularly Indian slate) is laminate-structured and will delaminate if soaked with water. Minimal moisture and immediate drying is essential.

Terrazzo (Traditional Cement vs Epoxy)

▼
Surface Category

Composite Stone — Marble/granite chips in cement (traditional) or epoxy (modern) binder. Polished. Critical distinction: cement and epoxy binders have very different chemical tolerances.

Approved — Traditional (Cement) Terrazzo
  • pH-neutral cleaner (7.0–9.0)
  • No acid, no strong alkali
Approved — Epoxy Terrazzo
  • pH 3–11 tolerated (more chemically resistant)
  • Mild alkaline degreaser acceptable
  • Mild acid descaler acceptable for mineral deposits (not on cement terrazzo)
Process
  1. Dust mop or auto-scrubber dry pass
  2. Auto-scrubber with appropriate pad (red: light scrub) or damp mop
  3. Rinse
  4. Dry buff for high polish
Auto-Scrubber Settings
  • Red pad (medium): routine cleaning
  • White pad: polish/buff
  • Never use black or brown stripping pads
Frequency

Daily damp mop; monthly machine scrub; repolish annually

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Using acid cleaner on traditional cement terrazzo attacks the binder matrix, not just the stone chips. Visible pitting occurs within one use. Restoration requires diamond grinding and repolishing — a $15–$30/sq ft repair on what is often a heritage floor.
  • Never assume terrazzo type without confirming with the facility. When in doubt, use pH-neutral only on both types.
  • Stripping pads (brown/black) used on terrazzo will remove the polish and gouge the surface. Machine work requires explicit supervisor approval and correct pad selection.
🏢 Hard Floors ▼

VCT — Vinyl Composition Tile (Strip & Wax Process)

▼
Surface Category

Synthetic Resilient Floor — Most common commercial floor tile. Requires periodic strip and wax cycle. Found in schools, hospitals, retail, warehouses.

Approved Chemicals
  • Daily: neutral floor cleaner pH 7.0–9.0, 1:64
  • Strip: alkaline stripper pH 12–13, undiluted or 1:4 for heavy buildup
  • Wax/finish: acrylic floor finish (water-based)
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Acidic cleaners — attacks finish layers
  • Oil-based soaps — prevents finish adhesion
  • Solvent-based products — dissolves VCT tile itself
Daily Cleaning Process
  1. Sweep or dust mop
  2. Damp mop with diluted neutral cleaner
  3. Allow to dry completely before foot traffic
Strip & Wax Process
  1. Wet floor with stripping solution, allow 10–15 min dwell
  2. Agitate with low-speed buffer (175 RPM), black stripping pad
  3. Extract slurry with wet vac
  4. Rinse floor 2–3× with clean water
  5. Neutralize with pH 7 rinse and allow to dry completely (30–60 min)
  6. Apply first coat of floor finish in thin, even strokes
  7. Dry 30–45 min between coats
  8. Apply 3–5 coats total
  9. Allow final coat to cure 2–4 hours before traffic
Equipment
  • Low-speed buffer 175 RPM: black pad (strip), red pad (scrub), white pad (buff)
  • High-speed burnisher 1500–2000 RPM: white/champagne pad (buff to gloss)
  • Wet vacuum for slurry removal
  • Microfiber flat mop for finish application
Frequency

Daily damp mop; scrub & recoat monthly; full strip & wax every 3–6 months

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Stripping solution left on VCT longer than 20 minutes begins to soften and warp the tile edges. Always work in sections on large floors.
  • Applying finish over incompletely stripped or poorly rinsed floor traps stripper residue, causing finish adhesion failure, bubbling, and yellowing within days.
  • Using a high-speed burnisher on a dirty floor without a prior damp mop will grind soil into the finish, causing deep scratching visible from any angle.

LVT / LVP — Luxury Vinyl Tile / Plank

▼
Surface Category

Synthetic Resilient Floor — Modern vinyl with wear layer. NO wax or finish required. Increasingly common in commercial and healthcare settings.

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral cleaner (7.0–9.0), 1:64
  • LVT-specific cleaner (manufacturer recommended)
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Wax or floor finish — NOT compatible. Buildup creates yellowing film that cannot be stripped without damaging the wear layer
  • Steam mops — heat softens adhesive layers and causes delamination
  • Bleach at high concentration (>5%)
  • Abrasive scrubbing pads
  • Solvent-based cleaners
Process
  1. Sweep or microfiber dust mop
  2. Damp mop with neutral solution — minimal water, especially on click-lock LVP (water seeps into seams)
  3. No-rinse formula preferred
  4. Air dry or dry mop
Equipment
  • Microfiber flat mop only
  • Auto-scrubber with soft pad (white/red) for large areas — low pressure setting
  • Never: rotary buffer, steam mop, wet mop (excess water)
Frequency

Daily damp mop; no stripping cycle needed; manufacturer recommends no machine scrubbing without approval

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Using a rotary buffer with a black stripping pad on LVT will delaminate the wear layer on contact. The damage is irreversible — a $15–$25/sq ft replacement cost for what was a routine cleaning task.
  • Applying any wax or acrylic floor finish to LVT creates a buildup film that looks like haze and yellows within months. The only removal method is aggressive chemical stripping which itself risks damaging the wear layer — a no-win situation.
  • Steam mops in break rooms or offices often get used on LVT. Heat above 140°F causes the adhesive to release, creating lifted edges and bubbling. Any crew-owned steam mops must not be used on LVT surfaces.

Hardwood (Sealed vs Unsealed)

▼
Surface Category

Natural Wood Floor. Sealed: polyurethane or similar topcoat — more cleaning-tolerant. Unsealed/oiled: bare or penetrating oil finish — much more vulnerable.

Approved — Sealed Hardwood
  • pH-neutral hardwood floor cleaner (7.0–8.5)
  • Hardwood-specific spray and flat mop system
  • Minimal moisture always
Approved — Unsealed/Oiled
  • Wood oil soap (Murphy Oil Soap or equivalent), diluted
  • Very lightly dampened cloth only
  • Annual re-oiling treatment
Prohibited — Both Types
  • Steam mops — moisture penetrates wood fibers, causes cupping and warping
  • Wet mopping — same result, slower but equally damaging
  • Vinegar/acid cleaners — degrades polyurethane finish
  • Ammonia cleaners — same
  • Alkaline floor cleaners (pH >10)
Process — Sealed
  1. Microfiber dust mop daily to remove grit
  2. Spray hardwood cleaner directly onto pad (not floor)
  3. Flat mop — barely damp, dry within 30 seconds of application
Frequency

Daily dust mop; damp clean weekly; professional refinish every 3–5 years (sealed)

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Wet mopping hardwood — even "just damp" — causes wood fibers to swell, then contract. Repeated over months this creates permanent cupping (edges rise, center dips). A full floor refinish costs $3–$8/sq ft. A replacement costs $10–$20/sq ft.
  • Using a steam mop on hardwood damages the finish in minutes and can warp planks permanently within a single session.
  • Oiled/unsealed hardwood is particularly common in higher-end office environments. Verify finish type before proceeding — when uncertain, use the most minimal method (dry dust mop only) and confirm with client.

Epoxy-Coated Concrete

▼
Surface Category

Industrial Floor — Concrete with applied epoxy coating. Common in warehouses, garages, commercial kitchens. Very durable but coating can be chemically degraded.

Approved Chemicals
  • Neutral to mildly alkaline cleaner (7.0–10.0)
  • Degreaser pH 9.0–10.5 for oil/grease (kitchen areas)
  • Dilute bleach for sanitizing (0.5–1%) if required
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Strong solvents (acetone, MEK) — dissolve epoxy coating
  • Concentrated acid — etches underlying concrete if coating fails
  • Citrus-based degreasers (d-limonene) — degrades epoxy binder
Process
  1. Sweep or blow debris
  2. Apply degreaser to grease spots, agitate with stiff brush
  3. Wet mop with neutral solution (can be wetter than stone floors)
  4. Scrub machine for large areas
  5. Rinse and allow to dry
Frequency

Daily sweep; damp mop as needed; inspect coating annually for chips/cracks and repair promptly

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Chips or cracks in the epoxy coating allow water and cleaning chemicals to penetrate to the concrete substrate, causing epoxy delamination. Any damage should be reported immediately — a small chip repaired promptly costs $50; a full recoat costs $3–$7/sq ft.
  • D-limonene degreasers (often marketed as "natural" or "citrus") are highly popular but are chemically incompatible with epoxy. They cause progressive softening and eventual peeling of the coating — typically noticed 2–3 months after regular use begins.

Polished Concrete

▼
Surface Category

Industrial/Commercial Floor — Mechanically ground and polished concrete, usually with densifier and/or guard applied. No topcoat. Increasingly common in retail, office, and hospitality.

Approved Chemicals
  • pH-neutral to mildly alkaline (7.0–10.0)
  • Concrete-specific neutral cleaner
  • Auto-scrubber with appropriate pad
Prohibited Chemicals
  • Acidic cleaners — etch polished surface
  • Wax or floor finish — incompatible with polished concrete
  • Bleach over 1% long-term — affects surface densifier
Process
  1. Dust mop — grit is the primary enemy
  2. Auto-scrubber or damp mop with neutral solution
  3. Dry buff with high-speed burnisher to maintain gloss
Frequency

Daily dust mop; weekly auto-scrub; monthly burnish; annual re-application of guard/densifier in high-traffic areas

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Grit and sand particles tracked onto polished concrete are abrasive against the polished surface. Not dust-mopping before wet cleaning embeds grit under the mop and creates scratch patterns visible under overhead lighting. Prevention is the only remedy — scratched polished concrete requires professional re-grinding.

Ceramic Tile (Glazed vs Unglazed)

▼
Surface Category

Fired Clay Tile. Glazed: glass surface layer — non-porous, easy clean. Unglazed: porous, matte, requires sealing.

Approved Chemicals
  • Glazed: pH 4–10 (flexible), neutral preferred daily
  • Unglazed: pH 7–9.5 only (porous, absorbs alkaline)
  • Tile-specific degreaser for kitchen/commercial kitchen tile
Prohibited — Unglazed
  • Wax — traps in pores, causes discoloration
  • Strong alkali on unsealed — penetrates and stains
Process
  1. Sweep/vacuum
  2. Damp mop with appropriate solution
  3. For grout: scrub with brush and neutral/mild alkaline cleaner
  4. Rinse thoroughly — residue in grout causes buildup
Frequency

Daily mop; grout scrub monthly; seal unglazed annually

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Applying wax to unglazed ceramic traps in the porous surface and discolors — yellowing appears within weeks. Professional stripping requires aggressive methods that risk tile damage.
  • Grout lines trap cleaning product residue easily — always rinse thoroughly after grout cleaning to prevent chemical buildup that attracts soil and discolors over time.

Porcelain Tile

▼
Surface Category

Dense Fired Tile — Lower porosity than ceramic, harder. Polished porcelain can mimic stone. Very common in commercial settings.

Approved Chemicals
  • pH 5–10 (robust surface tolerates wider range)
  • Neutral to mildly alkaline daily cleaner
  • Mild acid for mineral/hard water removal (periodic)
Prohibited
  • Wax on polished porcelain — creates hazy film
  • Abrasive scrubbers on polished finish — fine scratching
Process
  1. Sweep/vacuum
  2. Damp mop with neutral solution
  3. For mineral buildup: dilute acid cleaner (1:20), 5 min dwell, scrub, rinse thoroughly
  4. Buff polished porcelain dry for shine
Frequency

Daily mop; periodic descale in hard-water areas; no sealing required (sealed porcelain only for grout protection)

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Polished porcelain shows water spots and streaks readily — always buff dry after mopping in customer-facing areas or results look worse than before cleaning.
🛋 Soft Surfaces ▼

Commercial Carpet (Loop vs Cut Pile)

▼
Surface Category

Textile Floor — Loop pile: dense, harder surface, common in offices, hides soil. Cut pile: softer, more luxurious feel, shows footprints and soil faster. Nylon, olefin, or wool fiber types vary chemical tolerance.

Approved Chemicals
  • Neutral carpet extraction cleaner pH 7.0–9.5
  • Pre-spray spotter: pH 7–10 (test inconspicuous area)
  • Encapsulation cleaner for low-moisture cleaning
  • Enzyme-based spotters for organic soils (urine, food, blood)
Prohibited Chemicals
  • High-alkali spotters on wool carpet — wool is protein fiber, alkali causes irreversible damage
  • Bleach on colored carpet — removes dye permanently
  • Solvent spotters without ventilation — fumes and residue attract soil
  • Over-wetting — causes mold, backing delamination, odor
Process — Routine
  1. Vacuum thoroughly (HEPA filtration preferred)
  2. Pre-treat visible spots with appropriate spotter
  3. Agitate spots gently — blot, don't scrub (scrubbing spreads and deepens)
  4. Extraction cleaning or encapsulation clean
  5. Allow 2–4 hours dry time before heavy traffic
Loop vs Cut Pile Differences
  • Loop: More durable, resists crushing — standard equipment settings
  • Cut pile: Vacuum with beater bar set high to avoid fiber damage; pile direction matters for appearance
Frequency

Daily vacuum (high-traffic); deep extraction monthly (office); spot treat immediately; professional deep clean annually

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Over-wetting carpet during extraction leaves moisture trapped in the backing and subfloor. Visible mold growth occurs within 24–48 hours in humid climates. Backing delamination occurs over weeks. Use extraction machines set to maximum vacuum power and never leave carpet visibly wet.
  • Scrubbing a spot (rather than blotting) physically spreads the stain and drives it deeper into fibers. A 2-inch spill becomes a 6-inch stain with poor technique. Always blot from outside in.
  • Wool carpet with high-alkaline products: irreversible fiber degradation occurs with products above pH 10. Wool must always be identified before any chemical application.

Fabric Upholstery

▼
Approved
  • Water-based upholstery cleaner pH 7–9.5 (W or WS codes)
  • Dry cleaning solvent for S-coded fabrics
  • Enzyme spotter for organic stains
Prohibited
  • Water on S-coded fabric (solvent-only) — permanent water rings
  • Bleach
  • Alkaline degreaser — fiber damage
  • Rubbing — brush or blot only
Process
  1. Check fabric cleaning code (W, S, WS, or X)
  2. Vacuum with brush attachment
  3. Apply appropriate cleaner sparingly to cloth, not fabric
  4. Blot — never rub
  5. Allow to dry with airflow
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Applying water-based cleaner to an S-coded (solvent only) fabric will cause permanent water spotting or ring marks. Always check the cleaning code label under the cushion before proceeding.

Vinyl Upholstery

▼
Approved
  • Neutral all-purpose cleaner pH 7–9
  • Dilute bleach (0.5%) for sanitizing healthcare settings
  • Medical-grade disinfectant wipes where required
Prohibited
  • Solvent-based cleaners — soften and crack vinyl
  • Bleach concentration above 1% — discolors and dries vinyl
  • Abrasive scrubbers — scratch surface coating
Process
  1. Wipe with damp cloth and neutral cleaner
  2. For sanitizing: apply approved disinfectant, allow dwell time, wipe dry
  3. Apply vinyl conditioner monthly to prevent cracking
Frequency

Wipe daily in healthcare; weekly in office; condition monthly

⚠ Warning Flags
  • Vinyl upholstery in medical settings often gets cleaned with concentrated bleach wipes. Above 1% concentration, bleach dries and cracks the vinyl within months, requiring full replacement. Use approved healthcare-grade neutral disinfectants instead.
🪟 Glass & Mirrors ▼

Interior Glass / Windows & Mirrors

▼
Approved Chemicals
  • Ammonia-based glass cleaner (standard windows)
  • Alcohol-based cleaner for mirrors (streak-free)
  • Distilled water + white vinegar (1:1) for light maintenance
Prohibited
  • Ammonia-based cleaner on mirror backing edges — bleaches silver backing causing black edge spots
  • Abrasive pads — permanent scratches
  • Squeegee on tinted glass without technique training — streaking
Process
  1. Remove dust with dry microfiber first
  2. Apply cleaner to cloth/applicator — not directly to glass
  3. Wipe in overlapping passes (Z or S pattern)
  4. Follow with dry lint-free microfiber or squeegee (trained use)
Mirrors Specifically
  • Spray at center of mirror only
  • Do not spray edges — solution runs behind mirror, attacks silver backing
  • Wipe outward from center
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Ammonia or moisture reaching the edge of a mirror attacks the silver backing, creating black spots (foxing) that spread over time. These are permanent — the mirror must be replaced. Never spray directly onto a mirror.
  • Using glass cleaner near adjacent marble surfaces: if overspray lands on marble, the acidic or ammonia-containing mist can etch the surface. Protect marble surrounds before cleaning glass.

Tempered Glass

▼
Notes

Tempered glass cleans the same as standard glass, but requires extra care around edges and corners — tempered glass is strong in the center but shatters catastrophically if impacted at the edges. No scraping, no metal tools, no impact near edges.

Prohibited
  • Razor blades or metal scrapers (paint removal, etc.) — edge impact shatters panel
  • Metal cleaning tools
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Tempered glass panels in partition walls shatter entirely from a single edge impact. A razor blade slipping while removing a sticker can cause the entire panel to disintegrate — a $500–$2,000 replacement and potential injury. Use plastic scrapers only and avoid edge proximity.
🚽 Restroom Surfaces ▼

Porcelain Fixtures (Toilets, Urinals, Sinks)

▼
Approved Chemicals
  • Acid-based bowl cleaner (pH 1–3) for bowl interior — limescale/mineral removal
  • Neutral disinfectant (pH 7–9) for exterior surfaces
  • Quaternary ammonium or hydrogen peroxide disinfectant for high-touch areas
Prohibited
  • Bleach + acid bowl cleaner together — produces toxic chlorine gas. NEVER MIX.
  • Abrasive scouring powder on porcelain glaze — scratches provide harbor for bacteria
  • Strong alkali on chrome fittings (faucets, handles) — pits chrome
Process
  1. Apply bowl cleaner inside bowl first (allow to dwell while cleaning exterior)
  2. Clean exterior surfaces with disinfectant: tank, lid, seat, base, floor immediately around
  3. Scrub bowl with toilet brush, flush
  4. Wipe all exterior chrome and porcelain dry
  5. Log in compliance record (time, product, operator)
Dwell Time

Bowl cleaner: 5–10 min for heavy scale; disinfectant: per label (typically 60 sec contact)

Frequency

Daily in commercial settings; twice daily in high-use restrooms; document each clean

⚠ Warning Flags
  • NEVER mix acid bowl cleaners with bleach-based disinfectants. The reaction produces chlorine gas — a toxic inhalation hazard. This is the most common dangerous chemical accident in commercial cleaning. Establish a one-product-at-a-time protocol and train every crew member without exception.
  • Abrasive scouring pads on porcelain glaze create micro-scratches that harbor bacteria permanently, defeating the sanitation purpose. Use non-abrasive bowl brushes and soft pads only.

Stainless Steel Restroom Fixtures

▼
Approved Chemicals
  • Neutral cleaner or stainless-specific cleaner pH 7–9
  • Stainless steel polish for finish maintenance
  • Isopropyl alcohol for fingerprint removal
Prohibited
  • Bleach/chlorine products — causes pitting corrosion on stainless over time
  • Steel wool or abrasive pads — scratch surface, create corrosion sites
  • Acid cleaners (bowl cleaner, descalers) on stainless hardware
Process
  1. Wipe with damp microfiber with neutral cleaner
  2. Always wipe in direction of grain (visible brush lines)
  3. Dry immediately
  4. Apply stainless polish in direction of grain
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Wiping stainless steel against the grain creates visible scratch marks in the direction of cleaning — immediately obvious and unprofessional. Always identify the grain direction before cleaning and train crew to follow it consistently.
  • Bleach splashing onto stainless steel fixtures from bowl cleaning will cause pitting corrosion over months — gradual but irreversible.

Grout (Standard Cement vs Epoxy)

▼
Notes

Cement grout: porous, stains easily, requires sealing. Epoxy grout: non-porous, stain-resistant, nearly maintenance-free but scratches if abraded.

Cement Grout
  • Neutral to mildly alkaline cleaner (pH 7–10)
  • Grout brush required for deep cleaning
  • Apply penetrating grout sealer annually
  • Avoid acid — reacts with Portland cement, erodes grout
Epoxy Grout
  • Standard neutral cleaner
  • No acid needed (stain resistant)
  • No sealing required
  • Avoid abrasive pads — scratches surface
Process
  1. Pre-wet grout lines
  2. Apply cleaner, allow 3–5 min dwell
  3. Scrub with grout brush
  4. Rinse thoroughly
  5. For cement grout: seal after cleaning when dry
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Acid on cement grout erodes the binder progressively — over months of routine acid cleaning, grout lines visibly widen and crumble. The fix is full re-grouting.
  • Unsealed cement grout in restrooms absorbs cleaning chemical residue and body waste permanently, creating permanent discoloration and bacteria harboring that no amount of cleaning will resolve. Seal every 12 months minimum.
🏥 Medical / High-Touch Surfaces ▼

Medical-Grade Plastic (IV Poles, Bed Rails, Equipment Housings)

▼
Approved Chemicals
  • Quaternary ammonium disinfectant (hospital-grade, EPA registered)
  • Accelerated hydrogen peroxide products
  • Isopropyl alcohol 70% for spot sanitizing
  • Products with documented efficacy against target organisms (MRSA, C. diff, etc.)
Prohibited
  • Bleach at concentrations above 1,000 ppm (0.1%) on plastic — degrades material over time
  • Phenolic disinfectants near neonatal areas
  • Abrasive pads — create harboring scratches in plastic
  • Non-EPA-registered products — compliance failure risk
Process
  1. Clean visible soil first with neutral cleaner (clean before you disinfect)
  2. Apply disinfectant ensuring full surface coverage
  3. Allow full contact dwell time per label (typically 1–10 min)
  4. Wipe dry or allow to air dry per protocol
  5. Document product, lot number, time, operator
Compliance
  • Joint Commission (JC) requires documented cleaning protocol per surface type
  • EPA registration number must be recorded in SDS log
  • Crew must be trained and credentialed per facility policy
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Using non-EPA-registered disinfectants in healthcare settings is a Joint Commission compliance failure — facilities can lose accreditation. Every product used in a medical environment must have its EPA Reg. No. recorded. This is non-negotiable.
  • "Clean before disinfect" is not optional. Disinfectants cannot penetrate organic soil (blood, body fluid). Disinfecting without cleaning first provides false compliance and no actual microbial reduction.
  • Phenolic disinfectants near neonatal or NICU areas are contraindicated by most hospital protocols — always confirm facility-specific approved product list before cleaning any medical space.

Electronic Equipment Housings

▼
Approved
  • Pre-moistened electronics-safe wipes (isopropyl alcohol 70%, no bleach)
  • Slightly damp microfiber cloth with minimal moisture
  • Electronics-approved quaternary disinfectant wipes (for keyboards, touchscreens)
Prohibited
  • Spray liquids near electronics — fluid ingress causes irreversible damage
  • Bleach wipes on screens — strip anti-reflective coating
  • Ammonia-based products on touchscreens
Process
  1. Power down or sleep device where possible
  2. Apply product to cloth/wipe — never directly to device
  3. Wipe surfaces, avoid ports and gaps
  4. Allow to dry completely before powering on
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Spraying any liquid directly onto electronic equipment — even a single spray of glass cleaner — can cause permanent liquid damage to internal components. The damage may not be immediately visible but manifests hours later. Always apply to cloth, never to device.

Rubber & Silicone Surfaces

▼
Approved
  • Neutral cleaner pH 7–9
  • Isopropyl alcohol 70% for disinfecting
  • Mild soap solution
Prohibited
  • Petroleum-based solvents — swell and degrade rubber
  • Bleach at high concentration — degrades silicone over time
  • Abrasive pads — surface damage
  • Oil-based products on silicone
Process
  1. Wipe with neutral solution
  2. Disinfect with IPA wipe
  3. Air dry
✨ Metals ▼

Brushed Stainless Steel

▼
Approved
  • Stainless-specific cleaner with passivating agent
  • Neutral all-purpose cleaner pH 7–9
  • Microfiber cloth
Prohibited
  • Steel wool or iron-containing abrasives — embeds iron particles causing rust spots
  • Bleach — pitting corrosion
  • Acids — attacks passive layer
  • Cross-grain wiping — visible scratch marks
Process
  1. Identify grain direction
  2. Wipe with damp microfiber in grain direction
  3. Apply stainless cleaner/polish in grain direction
  4. Buff dry with clean microfiber
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Using steel wool on stainless steel embeds microscopic iron particles that cause orange rust spots within days — even on premium 316-grade stainless. Use only dedicated stainless pads or microfiber.

Brass (Lacquered vs Unlacquered)

▼
Lacquered Brass
  • Damp cloth with mild neutral cleaner only
  • Do not polish — polishing removes lacquer
  • Do not use brass polish on lacquered surfaces
Unlacquered Brass
  • Brass-specific cleaner or mild acid (lemon juice + salt for tarnish)
  • Brass polish applied with soft cloth
  • Rinse thoroughly after acid treatment
Both Types — Prohibited
  • Abrasive compounds — scratches
  • Bleach — green corrosion (verdigris)
  • Ammonia — tarnishes and corrodes
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Applying brass polish to lacquered brass removes the lacquer, exposing raw brass that then tarnishes rapidly. The only fix is professional re-lacquering. Always confirm with client whether brass is lacquered before any polish application.

Chrome & Aluminum

▼
Chrome
  • Neutral cleaner, soft cloth
  • Chrome-specific polish for shine
  • Prohibited: bleach (pitting), abrasive (scratching), strong acids
Aluminum
  • pH-neutral cleaner only
  • Prohibited: strong alkali (pH >10) — etches aluminum permanently, causes white oxide staining
  • Prohibited: strong acid — same result
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Alkaline degreasers (commonly used on floors) contact aluminum door frames and window tracks causing white etching stains within minutes. Protect aluminum surfaces from floor cleaning runoff.
🎯 Specialty Surfaces ▼

Whiteboards & Chalkboards

▼
Whiteboard
  • Use dry erase cleaner or isopropyl alcohol 70%
  • Microfiber cloth
  • For ghost marks: whiteboard conditioner or dry erase marker over ghost marks then wipe
  • Prohibited: abrasive pads, bleach, nail polish remover (acetone)
Chalkboard
  • Dry erase with felt eraser first
  • Damp sponge with water for deep clean
  • Allow to dry fully — chalk writes poorly on damp surfaces
  • Condition with chalk (prime board) after wet cleaning
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Using abrasive pads on whiteboards permanently scratches the coating, creating grooves that hold dry erase ink permanently — no amount of cleaning restores a scratched whiteboard surface.

Acoustic Ceiling Tiles

▼
Approved
  • Vacuum with soft brush attachment
  • Light damp sponge with neutral cleaner for spot cleaning only
  • Ceiling tile paint/coating spray for cosmetic restoration
Prohibited
  • Wet mopping or soaking — tiles warp, sag, and fall from grid
  • Pressure washing
  • Heavy scrubbing — destroys acoustic perforation pattern
⚠ Warning Flags
  • Acoustic ceiling tiles saturated with water sag and break under their own weight, falling from the grid. They are not recoverable — must be replaced. Never use anything wetter than a barely damp sponge, and even then only for spot treatment.

Fabric Wall Panels

▼
Approved
  • Vacuum with brush attachment (low suction)
  • Dry cleaning powder for spot treatment
  • Foam-based upholstery cleaner (W or WS coded panels)
Prohibited
  • Water-based cleaning on S-coded fabric
  • Rubbing — snags and pills fabric
  • Bleach — permanent color removal
Process
  1. Check fabric cleaning code
  2. Vacuum entire panel surface
  3. Spot treat with appropriate product
  4. Blot — never rub
  5. Air dry with good ventilation
